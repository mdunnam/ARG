# Somnatek ARG Story Breakdown

## The Complete Narrative

This document breaks down the full story of the Somnatek ARG from first contact through the infinite ongoing arc. It covers the world history, what actually happened, what players discover in sequence, and what the truth is that may never be fully stated.

---

## Part 1: The World History

### Before The Clinic: 1994-2006

The night floor exists before Somnatek does.

No one named it. No one mapped it. But for most of recorded history, a small percentage of people with sleep disorders reported a recurring institutional environment in their dreams. A hallway that bends left. A reception desk with no one at it. A room number they could not explain.

These reports appeared in medical literature as footnotes, dismissed as architectural familiarity, shared cultural imagery, or pattern-seeking behavior. No one connected them.

In 1994, a cognitive researcher named **Edwin Vale** at Wexler University encountered two sleep disorder patients who independently described the same window. The window looked into a room from the outside, from a floor that would have been above the room it was looking into.

He noted it, filed it, and did nothing for eleven years.

In 2005, Vale began the preliminary design for a sleep recall study. The official justification was standardizing dream recall assessment for clinical sleep medicine. The real motivation was the window.

### The Clinic Opens: 2006-2007

**Somnatek Sleep Health Center** opened in Harrow County in 2006.

Its medical director was **Dr. Mara Ellison**, a trained sleep medicine specialist with a strong clinical reputation and no apparent interest in anomalous research.

Ellison's public purpose: diagnostic sleep testing and CPAP compliance support.

Vale approached her in late 2006 with a research partnership proposal. He needed a clinical partner with a sleep lab to run a longitudinal recall study. Ellison agreed. The study was framed entirely as standard patient-reported dream research.

Neither of them expected anything unusual.

### The First Study: 2008-2010

The study began with forty-seven patients, all presenting with sleep disorders of various severity.

The protocol was simple: a short survey on waking, asking patients to describe what they remembered from sleep. No prompting, no imagery, no environment suggestions.

By Session 8, three patients had described the same hallway.

By Session 14, eleven patients had described the same reception desk.

By Session 22, a patient described a vending machine that only accepts room keys. No prior participant had used those words. Two other patients, who had not spoken to each other, had written the same phrase in their intake forms the previous week.

Vale identified this as the environmental recurrence event. He did not tell Ellison for six weeks.

### The Study Changes: 2010-2011

In early 2010, Vale proposed a protocol amendment. He wanted to introduce guided recall sessions, in which patients would be helped to re-enter the shared environment through audio cues and sleep monitoring.

Ellison approved it with conditions. The ethics board review was filed. The amendment was soft-pedaled in the filing as "supplemental environmental recall facilitation."

The ethics board approved it.

By mid-2010, the guided recall protocol was running. Patients were going to the same place more consistently. The clinic started calling the place an indexed space in internal documents.

Staff were given a checklist of environmental features to listen for in patient reports.

By late 2010, Lena Ortiz, a night shift sleep technician, noticed that patients were describing events that had not happened yet during their sessions.

She told no one. She started writing things down.

### The Protocol Deepens: 2011-2013

By 2011, the shared environment had a name inside the clinic: the Dorsal site. The choice of language was Vale's. Dorsal as in the back of something, the underside, the part you cannot see from the front.

The staff protocols were updated. Technicians were told not to ask leading questions. Certain language was removed from the informed consent documents. Patients were asked not to discuss their dream content with family members "to avoid cross-contamination of recall data."

Several patients reported finding each other inside the environment. Two patients who had never met described specific conversations in detail, then compared notes in the waiting room.

Vale published two conference abstracts during this period that described the study in neutral terms. The abstracts mentioned "high environmental recall consistency" without explaining what that meant.

Ellison became increasingly uncomfortable. She requested an internal review. Vale convinced her to wait until the study concluded.

By 2012, Lena Ortiz had filled three personal notebooks.

In her notes she wrote: "P-018 described the intake conversation I had with P-031 before I had it. The timestamps confirm this. I have not told anyone."

### The Study Ends: 2013

In September 2013, **Patient 041** submitted a recall form that included Room 413.

No room in the Somnatek building was numbered 413. No staff member had ever used that number. It had never appeared in any document.

Two weeks later, three other patients independently submitted forms with the same room number.

Ellison ordered the study suspended. Vale objected. He believed they were on the verge of something measurable, something publishable, something that would redefine the field.

He was removed from the active study but retained his academic affiliation.

Ellison began the closure process.

### The Closure: 2013-2014

The clinical study was formally terminated in early 2014.

The closure process involved patient notification letters, records transfer notices, and filing with the county business registry.

Patient files were transferred to **Dorsal Health Holdings**, a company registered two months after Somnatek's closure filing.

The registered agent for Dorsal Health Holdings was Edwin Vale.

The public Somnatek website was archived but not taken down. The records transfer notice went up. The staff directory was last updated in February 2014.

Lena Ortiz's position was eliminated. She did not receive a formal separation notice. Her name remained on the staff directory for seven months.

### After Closure: 2014-Present

Dorsal Health Holdings had no public presence. Its records showed one registered document: the patient file transfer agreement.

But appointment confirmation emails continued to go out. They came from a Somnatek address that should have been decommissioned.

Former patients reported receiving billing notices for sessions that had not taken place.

On the RestWell forum, former Somnatek patients began comparing notes. Most of the threads were mundane: CPAP maintenance, insurance complaints. But one thread titled "Somnatek follow-up, anyone else?" began accumulating replies.

Moderator actions on that thread began in 2016. Certain replies were edited or removed. The moderator account had no post history before 2015.

Patient 018, forum username **MarauderBlue**, posted in 2019: "I know this is going to sound like I need help. But I think I can go back there on my own now. I don't need to be asleep. I don't know how to explain that. If anyone who was at Somnatek reads this, please message me."

The thread was locked four days later.

His last post anywhere on the forum was in March 2020. It read: "Room 413. That's where they're all going. I mapped it."

The post was edited three minutes after it was published. The edit removed the words "I mapped it" and replaced them with a space.

## Part 2: What Players Experience

### Act 1: Arrival

The player finds the Somnatek site.

It looks like a normal dead medical portal. Most of the pages are functional. Sleep hygiene PDFs, insurance information, CPAP resources. The writing is clean and clinical. Nothing is immediately alarming.

The first things that create unease are small and deniable:

- The staff directory lists a Dr. Ellison with no photo and a note reading "on extended administrative leave."
- The closure notice mentions records transferred to Dorsal Health Holdings, which is not linked and returns no search results.
- A PDF download has a filename that includes the phrase `7A_INTERNAL_DO_NOT_DISTRIBUTE.pdf`. The file, when downloaded, appears to be a standard intake form. Most of it.

The research studies page is sparse. It describes a "longitudinal sleep recall study conducted between 2008 and 2013." There is a link to a summary document. The summary is clinical, unremarkable, and missing its final two pages.

The patient portal login page accepts input but returns: "This portal has been archived. Former patients may contact Dorsal Health Holdings for records access." There is no contact link for Dorsal Health Holdings.

### Act 2: The Recall Summaries

Finding the participant ID that works in the portal requires reading the study summary carefully.

The ID format appears in a data table footnote: `PTX-XXX`. The footnote says "participant identifiers assigned in study sequence." A partially visible table in the same PDF shows entries up to PTX-047 before the page ends.

Players test IDs. PTX-018 returns a portal page.

The portal is not a full patient profile. It is an archived set of recall summaries, formatted as scanned handwritten forms.

The forms are mundane at first. Patient reports dreams with typical imagery. Short responses. Clinical notation in the margin.

Then, around form 8, a margin note reads: "Corridor description consistent with P-007, P-014. Do not prompt."

By form 15, the sidebar checklist has new items: "Blue door: Y/N. Reception desk: Y/N. Bending hallway: Y/N."

By form 22: "Patient described vending machine accepting room keys. No prior exposure confirmed. Cross-reference P-011, P-031."

The forms grow shorter after that. The last one reads: "Patient requested discontinuation. Reports dreaming about this office."

### Act 2b: The Termination Reports (7A-SUPP-005)

The supplemental index lists documents 7A-SUPP-001 through 7A-SUPP-010. Five of them are not accessible. The index itself is a redirect page — it loads, pauses, and disappears before the player can read it. Enough remains in page source to extract the document list.

Among the accessible files: 7A-SUPP-005, the termination reports for the five Category 7 participants.

Each report follows the same structure: participant ID, session count, final checklist score, termination classification, and a brief disposition note. The clinical language is identical across all five. What is not identical is the detail that bleeds through:

- PTX-007: First unprompted corridor description (session 12). Note: "ongoing access confirmed in follow-up contact, June 2013."
- PTX-011: First unprompted vending machine mention (session 11). This is fourteen months before PTX-018's session 22 entry documenting the same detail.
- PTX-014: First participant to describe going through the blue door (session 22). The note reads: "reported light from everywhere. High ceiling. Hallway continuing."
- PTX-018: Room 413 named explicitly (session 20). Highest EEC consistency rating in the study. Withdrawal request submitted session 22.
- PTX-031: Active status. No termination rationale given.

PTX-011 mentioned the vending machine fourteen months before PTX-018 did. PTX-031 has no termination date. The Category 7 designation means these files are maintained as active — indefinitely, with scheduled recall at intervals set by the Archive Committee.

The player is reading their own intake intake paperwork before they know they are a participant.

### Act 3: The Protocol

Metadata in the PDF files contains author fields, edit histories, and internal tags.

The properties of the seventh summary document show an author field reading `L. Ortiz` and a last-modified date four months after the clinic officially closed.

Searching for Lena Ortiz in the forum returns nothing. Searching for her name in plain text inside the PDFs returns one result: a version of the staff protocol checklist that includes a line at the bottom reading "revised per LO note 3 - do not include room 413 in the standard environmental checklist."

The room number appears for the first time.

Protocol 7A is not immediately findable. It is linked from a hidden page index that requires combining three values from across the recall summaries: a session number, a participant ID, and a room number that appears only in the revised checklist.

When Protocol 7A loads, it is dense and bureaucratic. Most of it is standard sleep research language.

The final section, titled Recall Termination Criteria, includes this line:

> "If a participant reports an unprompted room designation of 413 prior to Session 12, discontinue intake and transfer file to archive category 7. Do not schedule follow-up. Do not issue closure notice. Maintain record as active."

### Act 4: The Forum

The RestWell forum is a separate site. Players find it through a Somnatek forum signature embedded in a cached version of the staff directory.

The forum looks genuinely old. Most threads are irrelevant to the ARG.

The thread "Somnatek follow-up, anyone else?" is on page 3. It has 87 replies.

Mapping usernames to participant IDs requires cross-referencing post dates against the session schedule in the recall summary table. MarauderBlue joined the forum two months after session logs place PTX-018's final clinic appointment.

The deleted thread is not fully gone. Fragment reconstruction using reply quotes, thread quote blocks, and an archived preview link reveals the content of MarauderBlue's final post.

The full reconstruction reads:

> "Room 413. That's where they're all going. I mapped it. You go left down the main corridor. You go left again. The vending machine will be on your right. Don't use it. Go past it. The blue door will be on your left. Don't open it. Keep going until the hallway ends at a window. Room 413 is the room on the other side of that window. You will not be able to enter it from the hall. You have to go around. I have been there sixteen times since 2014. I did not need to be asleep for the last eleven. If anyone who was at Somnatek reads this, you already know what I mean."

The edit timestamp is three minutes after publication. The post history shows an edit by user account `portal-mod`, which has no public profile, joined in 2015, no post history.

### Act 5: The Corporate Record

County business records show Somnatek Sleep Health Center registered in 2006, operating under its legal name until October 2013, then reregistered under the name Somnatek Sleep Health Center LLC in November 2013.

Two months after the closure filing, Dorsal Health Holdings LLC was registered. The registered agent: Edwin Vale.

The patient file transfer agreement is a county-recorded document. Its text is mostly standard. One section reads:

> "Active recall files, as defined in exhibit C, are excluded from standard archive and destruction timelines. Active recall files will be maintained in perpetuity under the care of the receiving entity."

Exhibit C is not attached to the public record.

Cross-referencing the document ID number with the Somnatek records page returns a page that should not exist: a maintenance upload index dated 2022.

The index shows one file: `active-recall-manifest-current.pdf`.

The PDF loads. It is a list of participant IDs. At the bottom of the column, below PTX-047, there are new entries. They begin at VIS-001.

The entries at the top of the VIS column are dated 2015 through 2023.

The most recent entry is dated within the last thirty days.

### Act 6: Room 413

The final Season 1 page requires combining the recall termination criteria, the corporate record date, and the room number sequence from MarauderBlue's reconstructed post.

The page loads as a standard Somnatek portal status screen.

At the top: "APPOINTMENT CONFIRMED."

Below that:

> Room: 413
>
> Arrival window: 02:10 – 02:17
>
> Facility: Dorsal Health Holdings, active recall division
>
> Assigned observer: [visitor designation]
>
> If recalled early, remain seated.
>
> If you wake before arrival, do not re-enter the hallway.
>
> Your file has been updated. Thank you for your continued participation.

## Part 3: What Is Actually True

This section contains the confirmed truth of the Somnatek world. It should never be fully stated inside the ARG.

### The Night Floor Is Real

The shared environment exists. It is not a collective hallucination or a product of conditioning.

The Somnatek study did not create it. It found it. Patients had been accessing it independently for years before the study. The study provided consistent enough access that the environment could be mapped.

The environment is not dangerous in any obvious way. It does not harm people who enter it.

What it does is harder to define. The people who spend significant time in it begin to experience it outside of sleep.

### Room 413 Is The Stable Point

Most of the environment shifts slightly between visits. Room positions change. The hallway bends in directions that should not be geometrically possible.

Room 413 does not move.

It is always at the end of the same route MarauderBlue described: left, left, past the vending machine, blue door on the left, hallway end, window.

The room cannot be entered from the hallway. It can only be entered from around.

No patient or staff member has ever described the interior of Room 413. Every file that references it describes approaching it, seeing it through the window, or being told not to enter.

### The Clinic Is Still Running

Dorsal Health Holdings is not dormant.

The appointment confirmations are real within the fiction. The active recall manifest is being updated.

The question the ARG will never fully answer is who is updating it, and whether the clinic staff or their successors are doing so intentionally, or whether the environment itself has developed the capacity to schedule arrivals.

### Lena Ortiz Left Warnings

Lena Ortiz spent years leaving evidence.

Her file modifications, her notation patterns, her edited PDFs, her partial participation in the forum under a secondary username are all breadcrumbs pointing toward the same conclusion: the environment does not only receive visitors. At some point in the later sessions, it started sending information back.

Her final known communication is a metadata field in one of the recall summary scans. The field reads:

> "If you found this, you are already in the manifest. I am sorry."

## Part 4: Character Arcs In Full

### Dr. Mara Ellison

**The arc of someone who did the wrong thing for the right reason, then the right thing too late.**

2006: Opens the clinic. Genuinely wants to help sleep disorder patients.

2008: Agrees to the recall study. Purely academic. No red flags.

2010: Reviews early cross-patient reports. Finds them scientifically interesting. Approves protocol amendment with ethics filing.

2011: Begins to feel uncomfortable. The patients are describing staff conversations.

2012: Requests internal review. Vale convinces her to wait.

2013: Patient 041 submits Room 413 and Ellison shuts it down.

2014: Transfers files to Dorsal Health Holdings without fully reading exhibit C. She believes she is protecting patients.

Post-2014: Disappears from public records. On extended administrative leave, per the staff directory, indefinitely.

Her arc is a story about institutional complicity. She never intended harm. She approved harm one small step at a time.

### Dr. Edwin Vale

**The arc of someone who stopped treating people as people.**

2005: Identifies the shared window. Files a note and waits.

2008: Launches the recall study. Genuinely excited.

2010: Proposes the protocol amendment. Stops thinking of patients as individuals.

2011-2013: Uses increasingly abstracted language in internal documents. "Environmental subjects." "Indexed participants." "Recall units."

2013: Loses access to the active study when Ellison shuts it down.

2014: Registers Dorsal Health Holdings. Transfers the active recall files.

Post-2014: Continues. The ongoing operation is his. Whether he is mapping the environment, trying to enter it himself, or has crossed some further threshold is unclear and should remain so.

His last known public appearance is a 2019 university emeritus listing. His profile photo was removed at an unknown date.

### Lena Ortiz

**The arc of someone who saw the truth clearly and was completely powerless.**

2008: Hired as a night shift sleep technician.

2010: Starts noticing discrepancies between patient reports and session timelines.

2011: First notes patient describing an event before it happened.

2012: Keeps three notebooks. Starts hiding notes inside official documents.

2013: Realizes she cannot report this internally. Starts leaving external evidence trails.

2014: Position eliminated. No formal notice.

Post-2014: Forum participation, PDF metadata, hidden file notes. She is the ARG's conscience. Her warnings are everywhere if players know to look.

Current status: Unknown. No forum posts since 2021. Her secondary forum account was last active in late 2020. Her metadata note is timestamped from 2022.

### Patient 018 / MarauderBlue / Caleb R.

**The arc of someone who went too far in and cannot come back.**

2009: Enrolled in the study for sleep apnea.

2011-2013: Regular participant. Reports increasing consistency with the shared environment.

2014: Clinic closes. Receives no explanation.

2015: Joins RestWell forum. Starts cautiously asking other former patients.

2016-2019: Posts regularly. Grows more specific.

2019: Posts the full room 413 route. Edited three minutes later.

2020: Final post. Gone.

Current status: No online presence. His visitor ID in the active recall manifest is listed as VIS-009. Status field: "recurring."

He did not become a victim of the environment. He became fluent in it.

The distinction may not be comforting.

### Patient 041

**The arc that has not been told yet.**

Patient 041 submitted Room 413 before any staff had used that designation.

Their intake file is the most redacted in the archive. All that is visible is the session log header, two ambiguous initials, and the room number.

Every other document that references 041 does so obliquely or not at all.

The only character who mentions them directly is Lena Ortiz, in a notebook margin note visible in one of the scanned files:

> "P-041 was not dreaming it. P-041 was reporting from there. Present tense. The forms are past tense for everyone else."

What this means is not explained. It is the thread players will pull at for years.

## Part 5: The Long Ongoing Story

### What Happens After Season 1

Season 1 ends with the appointment confirmation. The player is in the manifest.

Season 2 begins with the discovery that Dorsal Health Holdings had successors.

Multiple clinics, different states, different names, all affiliated with Wexler University's cognitive science department through shell grants.

All of them used variations of Protocol 7A.

All of them stopped public operations between 2014 and 2018.

All of them had an active recall manifest.

The manifests are all keyed to the same room number.

### What Escalates

Each arc beyond Season 1 should shift the player's perceived role:

Season 1: Investigator.

Season 2: Classified. The player has a designation.

Season 3: Recurring. The player is on the active manifest.

Season 4: Contributing. The player's interactions are producing new documents.

Season 5: Recalled. The appointments are not confirmations. They are records of something that has already happened.

### What Is Never Explained

- What the night floor is or where it exists.
- Whether Room 413 can be entered, and by whom.
- Whether Patient 041 is still there.
- Whether Edwin Vale entered the environment himself.
- Whether Lena Ortiz is still leaving warnings or whether she stopped for a reason.
- Whether the environment is passive or intentional.
- Whether any of the appointment confirmations correspond to something real.

The ARG can run indefinitely because the central question is not answerable by definition.

The environment exists. It can be visited. It cannot be understood.

That ambiguity is the engine.
